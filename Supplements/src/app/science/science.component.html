Nervous System

Dopaminergic System

A group of nerve cells, most of which originate in the midbrain.

They send their axons to different parts of the forebrain, plugging into particular functions. There are three main branches of the dopamine system:
  • One goes all the way to the frontal cortex, modulating cognitive function and enhances the efficiency of certain forms of thinking and working memory.
  • One goes to the striatum, involved in facilitating movements. Without dopamine, your movements become rigid and reduced in number and amplitude.
  • One goes to structures in the limbic system (emotional center of the brain), including the nucleus accumbens (reward center). More dopamine induces a greater effect from the reward system.
With this being said, dopamine is important for thinking optimally, moving optimally, and motivating yourself. With the latter, you can tailor what the reward the dopamine is inciting by associating its onset with a particular action or thought.

L-Dopa is dopamine's precursor.

Serotonergic System

Originating in the midbrain, this system seems to be very involved in inhibition, meaning that without it, the brain is inhibited.

Little is known about the precise functions of serotonin. However, it is known that without enough or with too much of it, the brain is not healthy, and one would most likely be diagnosed with a particular mental disorder.
Serotonin is known as being responsible for overall feelings of happiness and meaning.

5-HTP is serotonin's precursor.

Noradrenergic System

Involves norepinephrine (noradrenaline) and epinephrine (adrenaline).

Originates in brain stem structures such as the locus ceruleus, as well as the medulla. There are two main branches:
  • One is called the dorsal ascending system, which projects all over the forebrain, including the neocortex and the hippocampus. It does not go to the striatum, however.
  • The other is the ventral bundle, which mainly projects to the hypothalamus and to other visceral parts of the brain including the limbic system.
Overall, this system is involved in arousal. It is least active when you’re asleep, and most active when you are thinking or moving intensely. The experience of novelty makes this system work very fast. Too much novelty can cause this system to act out of balance, causing undue anxiety.

Dopamine is a precursor to norepinephrine.

Glutamate System

One of the major amino acid systems (GABA being the other one), the glutamate system is a fast signaling system crucial for information processing in neuronal networks of the neocortex and the hippocampus in particular.

It is very involved in the process of long-term potentiation, which is a neuronal model of memory. There are three major types of glutamate receptors:
  • AMPA receptor
  • NMDA receptor
  • Kainate receptor
Some people think that we will discover memory-enhancing drugs by using agents that work at these receptors, such as ampakines.

Glutamine is glutamate's precursor.

Cholinergic System

Comprised of cholinergic cells, which accumulate and release acetylcholine.

They are involved in a particular form of arousal, called vigilance. Acetylcholine is a neurotransmitter that, when released, sends signals to other cells (such as neurons, muscle cells, and gland cells). It can be thought of as a messenger neurotransmitter. It exists in two main branches:
  • One branch projects to the neocortex
  • One branch projects to the hippocampus
They are greatly involved in enhancing signal to noise ratios, enabling you to detect targets with great fidelity. They also contribute to memory-encoding processes and working-memory processes by enhancing the power of the signal going to these processes.

Choline or L-Acetylcholine is Acetylcholine’s precursor.
